1 Why Prompt Quality Matters

The Same AI Can Produce Very Different Results

One of the biggest misconceptions about AI is that output quality depends only on the model. In reality:

The same AI model can generate dramatically different outputs depending on the quality of the prompt.

A prompt acts like a set of instructions given to an employee. The clearer the instructions, the more useful the result. Ambiguity is the enemy of quality output.

What Research Consistently Shows

Industry guidance from OpenAI, Anthropic, and other AI providers repeatedly highlights four factors that improve output quality:

  1. Clear context — background information the AI needs
  2. Specific instructions — precise task description
  3. Defined output format — structure and length requirements
  4. Examples of desired responses — models to follow

Prompts containing these elements consistently outperform vague requests. The point is not that the AI becomes smarter — the point is that ambiguity decreases.

Enterprise Observation: Prompt Length vs. Quality

A community experiment tracking over 200 prompt-output pairs found a clear correlation between prompt length and output quality. The biggest factor was not a fancy framework — it was providing enough business context.

Prompt LengthAverage Quality RatingKey Characteristic
Under 50 words5.2 / 10Vague, no context, generic output
50 – 150 words7.1 / 10Some structure, partial context
Over 150 words8.4 / 10Rich context, clear format, examples

Note: This was a community experiment, not a peer-reviewed study, but it aligns with enterprise prompt-engineering guidance from major AI providers.

Result: More relevant · More structured · Less rework required · Easier to use in business communication

The Cost of Poor Prompts

Poor prompts often lead to:

  • Multiple re-prompts and iterations
  • More review time and manual editing
  • Missing information and gaps
  • Inconsistent outputs across requests
  • Increased risk of hallucination (fabricated information)

A good prompt reduces iterations and helps the model focus on the task at hand.

💡 Garbage In → Garbage Out
AI models are powerful, but they cannot infer business context that was never provided. Better prompts reduce ambiguity, improve consistency, and produce more actionable outputs.

2 Understanding Large Language Models

What Is a Large Language Model?

A Large Language Model (LLM) is an AI system trained on vast amounts of text data to understand and generate human-like language. Popular LLMs include ChatGPT (OpenAI), Claude (Anthropic), Gemini (Google), and Copilot (Microsoft).

Think of an LLM as an extremely well-read assistant who has studied billions of documents — including books, articles, websites, and professional literature. When you ask a question or give an instruction, the model draws on patterns learned from this training to generate a relevant response.

The model does not search the internet in real-time (unless specifically enabled) or access your organization's systems. It generates responses based on patterns in its training data, which has a knowledge cutoff date. This is why verification of facts remains important.

Key Characteristics of LLMs

CharacteristicDescriptionImplication for Auditors
Probabilistic Output Responses are generated word-by-word based on probability Same prompt may yield slightly different outputs; always review results
Context Window Limited memory within a conversation (measured in tokens) Very long documents may need to be processed in sections
Knowledge Cutoff Training data has an end date; no real-time information Verify current policies, regulations, and organizational changes
No True Understanding Pattern matching, not genuine comprehension May produce plausible-sounding but incorrect information
Prompt Sensitivity Output quality depends heavily on input quality Well-crafted prompts yield significantly better results

Capabilities & Limitations

✅ What LLMs Do Well:

  • Drafting and editing written content (reports, memos, findings)
  • Summarizing lengthy documents and extracting key points
  • Brainstorming and identifying risks, controls, and test procedures
  • Explaining complex concepts in plain language
  • Structuring information (converting notes to organized formats)
  • Generating interview questions and discussion guides
  • Comparing documents against criteria or policies

❌ What LLMs Cannot Do:

  • Access your organization's systems, databases, or files (unless specifically connected)
  • Perform calculations with guaranteed accuracy (always verify math)
  • Replace professional judgment on complex audit matters
  • Guarantee factual accuracy (may "hallucinate" false information)
  • Understand context you have not provided
  • Make ethical judgments or exercise professional skepticism on your behalf
⚠️ CRITICAL — Hallucinations: LLMs can generate confident-sounding but completely false information. This is called "hallucination." Always verify factual claims, especially citations to specific policies, regulations, or standards. The model may invent policy numbers, regulation sections, or audit standards that do not exist.

Security & Confidentiality Considerations

DATA PROTECTION REQUIREMENTS: Before using any AI tool with audit information, understand your organization's policies on:

  1. What data can be entered into AI systems
  2. Whether enterprise or consumer versions are approved
  3. Data retention and training policies of the AI provider
  4. Handling of confidential, sensitive, or personally identifiable information (PII)

Best Practices for Confidential Information:

  • Remove or anonymize names, account numbers, and identifying details before pasting into AI tools
  • Use enterprise versions with appropriate data handling agreements when available
  • Never enter passwords, social security numbers, or financial account details
  • Consider whether AI output could be discoverable in legal proceedings
  • Check if your organization requires AI use to be documented in workpapers

When in doubt, anonymize data before using AI tools.

3 Prompt Engineering Fundamentals

What Is Prompt Engineering?

Prompt engineering is the skill of crafting effective instructions (prompts) to get useful outputs from AI systems. A well-designed prompt clearly communicates what you need, provides relevant context, and guides the AI toward the desired format and quality of response.

Think of prompting as giving instructions to a highly capable but literal-minded assistant. The more specific and clear your instructions, the better the results. The assistant won't guess what you mean — it will follow exactly what you say.

The Anatomy of an Effective Prompt

Effective prompts typically include several key components that work together to guide the AI:

ComponentPurposeExample
Role / Persona Sets expertise level and perspective "Act as an experienced internal auditor..."
Context Provides background information "I am planning an audit of accounts payable at a public university..."
Task Clearly states what you need "Identify 8–10 inherent risks organized by category..."
Format Specifies desired output structure "Present as a numbered list with risk descriptions and ratings..."
Constraints Sets boundaries or requirements "Focus on risks specific to higher education. Maximum 500 words."
Examples Shows desired output style "Similar to: Risk: Duplicate payments. Likelihood: Medium. Impact: High."

The CRAFT Framework for Prompt Writing

Use the CRAFT framework to structure effective prompts. Each letter represents a critical element:

C

Context

What background does the AI need?

Provide relevant background information the AI needs to understand your situation. Include your role, the engagement type, the organization type, and any relevant constraints.

Example: "I am a staff auditor at a public university conducting an assurance engagement on procurement card (P-Card) controls. The university has approximately 500 cardholders with a total annual spend of $8 million."
R

Role

What expertise should guide the response?

Assign a role or persona to guide the AI's perspective and expertise level. This helps calibrate the response appropriately for your needs.

Example: "Act as an experienced internal auditor with expertise in higher education and procurement controls."
A

Action

What specific task should be performed?

Clearly state the specific task or action you want the AI to perform. Use action verbs and be specific about deliverables. Ambiguity here leads to generic responses.

Example: "Develop five testing procedures to evaluate whether P-Card transactions comply with the university's $2,500 single-transaction limit."
F

Format

How should the output be structured?

Specify how you want the output structured. This might include format type, length, sections, or specific elements to include. The more precise, the less editing needed.

Example: "For each procedure, include: (1) procedure objective, (2) population definition, (3) sample size with rationale, (4) specific steps, (5) pass/fail criteria."
T

Tone

What style is appropriate for the audience?

Indicate the appropriate tone, style, or audience for the output. Consider whether the content is for internal use, management presentation, or board reporting.

Example: "Use professional audit language suitable for inclusion in a formal audit report. Avoid jargon when possible."

💡 CRAFT in One Prompt

Role: Act as an experienced internal auditor with expertise in higher education. Context: I am planning an audit of P-Card controls at a public university with 500 cardholders and $8M annual spend. Task: Develop five testing procedures to evaluate compliance with the $2,500 single-transaction limit. Format: For each procedure, include objective, population definition, sample size, specific steps, and pass/fail criteria. Tone: Use professional audit language suitable for a formal audit report.

Common Prompting Mistakes to Avoid

MistakeProblemBetter Approach
Too vague "Write something about controls" "Write a control description for the three-way match process in accounts payable"
No context "Is this a good control?" "Given this control [description] designed to prevent [risk], evaluate its design adequacy"
Too many tasks "Write finding, recommendation, get management response, and rate the risk" Break into separate prompts for each task
Assuming knowledge "Update the finding per our discussion" Provide the specific changes needed; AI has no memory of your discussion
No format guidance "Give me some interview questions" "Provide 10 open-ended interview questions in numbered list format"

Variables & Placeholders

The prompt library uses placeholder variables in double curly braces {{like_this}} to indicate where you should insert engagement-specific information. This makes prompts reusable across different audits.

Common variables include:

VariableDescriptionVague ExampleSpecific Example
{{audit_area}} The process, department, or system being audited "payroll" "payroll processing for 2,500 employees including faculty, staff, and student workers with bi-weekly and monthly pay cycles"
{{process_name}} Specific business process name "procurement" "Purchase Requisition to Payment process using Banner ERP"
{{organization_type}} Type of organization "university" "public R1 research university with a medical center and 35,000 students"
{{risk_description}} Description of the specific risk "fraud" "unauthorized vendor creation and payment to fictitious vendors due to lack of segregation of duties"
{{audit_period}} Time period covered by the engagement "FY24" "July 1, 2024 through December 31, 2024 (first half of fiscal year)"
💡 TIP — Be Specific with Variables: When replacing variables, provide enough detail for the AI to give a useful response. Specificity in variables directly translates to quality in outputs. The AI cannot fill in gaps you haven't provided.
4 Quality Assurance & Professional Judgment

The Human-AI Partnership

AI is a tool to augment, not replace, professional judgment. The internal auditor remains responsible for all work product quality, regardless of AI assistance. Think of AI as a capable research assistant who can draft content quickly but needs your expert review and refinement.

Auditor Responsibilities When Using AI:

  • Verify all factual claims, especially policy citations and regulatory references
  • Ensure outputs align with your organization's specific context and requirements
  • Apply professional skepticism to AI suggestions just as you would to management assertions
  • Maintain independence — AI recommendations do not substitute for your professional judgment
  • Document AI use appropriately per department policies

Quality Review Checklist for AI Outputs

Before using any AI-generated content in your audit work, verify each of the following:

  • ✔ Accuracy: Are all facts, policy references, and standards citations correct? Research and correct — AI may have hallucinated.
  • ✔ Completeness: Does the output address all aspects of the request? Use follow-up prompt or supplement manually.
  • ✔ Relevance: Is the content specific to my organization and engagement? Customize generic content; remove irrelevant items.
  • ✔ Objectivity: Is the language neutral and free from bias? Edit for professional tone; remove opinions.
  • ✔ Clarity: Will the intended audience understand this? Simplify language; define technical terms.
  • ✔ Consistency: Does this align with other engagement documentation? Reconcile differences; ensure coherent narrative.

Documenting AI Use

Organizations may have varying requirements for documenting AI assistance. At minimum, consider:

  • Noting in workpapers when AI tools were used for significant tasks
  • Retaining significant prompts and outputs if required by policy
  • Documenting the verification steps performed on AI output
  • Identifying which portions of deliverables were AI-assisted vs. original work

Avoiding Over-Reliance on AI

PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT CONCERN: While AI can accelerate work, over-reliance may hinder professional development. New auditors should:

  • Understand the methodology behind prompts before using them
  • Attempt tasks manually occasionally to build foundational skills
  • Use methodology notes as learning opportunities
  • Discuss AI outputs with supervisors to calibrate judgment

Supervisor Review Considerations

Supervisors reviewing AI-assisted work should:

  • Ask how AI was used and what verification was performed
  • Spot-check factual claims, especially policy and regulation references
  • Ensure the auditor can explain and defend the work product
  • Verify that professional judgment was applied, not just AI output accepted
  • Coach on effective AI use as part of professional development
★ Key Takeaways

Remember These Principles

🎯

Be Specific

Vague prompts produce generic outputs. Specific instructions produce targeted results.

📋

Use CRAFT

Context, Role, Action, Format, Tone — five elements that transform average prompts into excellent ones.

🔍

Always Verify

AI can hallucinate. Every factual claim must be checked before it enters your workpapers.

👤

You Are Responsible

AI augments your work but never replaces your professional judgment and skepticism.

Final Thought: Prompt engineering is not about learning a complex technical skill — it's about communicating clearly. The same skills that make you an effective auditor (precision, skepticism, thoroughness) make you an effective prompt engineer. Every word in your prompt matters, just as every piece of evidence in your workpapers matters.
