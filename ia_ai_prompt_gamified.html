Evolution

The Audit Profession's Journey with Technology

Generative AI is the latest — and most transformational — shift in a decades-long evolution of audit technology.

1980s — Spreadsheets
Replaced paper ledgers. Enabled auditors to perform calculations and create working papers electronically for the first time.
1990s — Computer-Assisted Audit Techniques (CAATs)
Tools like ACL and IDEA allowed auditors to analyze large datasets, run queries, and test entire populations instead of samples.
2010s — Data Analytics & Visualization
Dashboards, continuous monitoring, and advanced analytics transformed how auditors detected anomalies and communicated findings.
2024+ — Generative AI & Large Language Models
Unlike previous tools that required structured data, LLMs understand natural language, analyze unstructured text, and assist with complex judgment-intensive tasks. This changes everything.

What is an LLM?

Your Extremely Well-Read AI Assistant

Think of an LLM as an assistant who has studied billions of documents — books, articles, websites, professional literature — and uses patterns from that training to help you. It does not search the internet in real-time or access your organization's systems unless specifically connected to do so.

Characteristic What It Means Implication for Auditors
Probabilistic Output Responses generated word-by-word based on probability Same prompt may yield slightly different outputs — always review results
Context Window Limited memory within a conversation (measured in tokens) Very long documents may need to be processed in sections
Knowledge Cutoff Training data has an end date; no real-time information Verify current policies, regulations, and organizational changes
No True Understanding Pattern matching, not genuine comprehension May produce plausible-sounding but incorrect information
Prompt Sensitivity Output quality depends heavily on input quality Well-crafted prompts yield significantly better results
⚠️
CRITICAL: Hallucinations
LLMs can generate confident-sounding but completely false information — this is called "hallucination." Always verify factual claims, especially citations to specific policies, regulations, or standards. The model may invent policy numbers, regulation sections, or audit standards that do not exist.
Capabilities

What LLMs Can and Cannot Do

Setting realistic expectations is key to using AI effectively in your audit work.

✅ LLMs Do Well
  • Drafting and editing written content (reports, memos, findings)
  • Summarizing lengthy documents and extracting key points
  • Brainstorming and identifying risks, controls, and test procedures
  • Explaining complex concepts in plain language
  • Structuring information (converting notes to organized formats)
  • Generating interview questions and discussion guides
  • Comparing documents against criteria or policies
❌ LLMs Cannot Do
  • Access your organization's systems, databases, or files (unless connected)
  • Perform calculations with guaranteed accuracy — always verify math
  • Replace professional judgment on complex audit matters
  • Guarantee factual accuracy — may "hallucinate" false information
  • Understand context you have not provided in your prompt
  • Make ethical judgments or exercise professional skepticism on your behalf
Security

Data Protection & Confidentiality

Before using any AI tool with audit information, you must understand your organization's policies.

🔒
DATA PROTECTION REQUIREMENTS
Understand: (1) What data can be entered into AI systems, (2) Whether enterprise or consumer versions are approved, (3) Data retention and training policies of the AI provider, (4) Handling of confidential, sensitive, or personally identifiable information (PII). When in doubt, anonymize data before using AI tools.
🔐
Anonymize Before Sharing
Remove or anonymize names, account numbers, and identifying details before pasting any information into AI tools.
🏢
Use Enterprise Versions
Use enterprise versions with appropriate data handling agreements when available. Consumer versions may use your data for model training.
⚖️
Consider Legal Exposure
Consider whether AI output could be discoverable in legal proceedings. Check if your department requires AI use to be documented in workpapers.
🚫
Never Enter Sensitive IDs
Never enter passwords, social security numbers, or financial account details into any AI tool under any circumstances.

The Framework

Master the CRAFT Framework

Click each letter to explore the component in depth. CRAFT is your go-to structure for writing effective AI prompts every time.

C
Context
Background the AI needs to understand your situation
R
Role
Expertise perspective to guide the AI's response
A
Action
The specific task you want performed
F
Format
How you want the output structured
T
Tone
Style appropriate for your audience
C — Context: Provide relevant background information the AI needs to understand your situation. Include your role, the engagement type, the organization type, and any relevant constraints.

Example: "I am a staff auditor at a public university conducting an assurance engagement on procurement card (P-Card) controls. The university has approximately 500 cardholders with a total annual spend of $8 million."

Pro Tip: More context = better output. Don't assume the AI knows your organization, its size, its industry, or its specific processes. Tell it everything relevant.
R — Role: Assign a role or persona to guide the AI's perspective and expertise level. This calibrates the response appropriately.

Example: "Act as an experienced internal auditor with expertise in higher education and procurement controls."

Pro Tip: The role sets the "lens" through which the AI interprets your request. "Act as a fraud examiner" will give you a very different perspective than "Act as a compliance officer."
A — Action: Clearly state the specific task or action you want the AI to perform. Use action verbs and be specific about deliverables.

Example: "Develop five testing procedures to evaluate whether P-Card transactions comply with the university's $2,500 single-transaction limit."

Pro Tip: Use verbs like: Develop, Draft, Identify, Analyze, Evaluate, Summarize, Create, List, Compare. Avoid vague verbs like "help with" or "tell me about."
F — Format: Specify how you want the output structured — format type, length, sections, or specific elements to include.

Example: "For each procedure, include: (1) procedure objective, (2) population definition, (3) sample size with rationale, (4) specific steps, (5) pass/fail criteria."

Pro Tip: Numbered lists, tables, bullet points, specific word counts, headings — the more precise your format instruction, the more usable the output will be for your workpapers.
T — Tone: Indicate the appropriate tone, style, or audience for the output. Consider whether content is for internal use, management presentation, or board reporting.

Example: "Use professional audit language suitable for inclusion in a formal audit report. Avoid jargon when possible."

Pro Tip: Different audiences need different language. "Executive summary for the Board" vs "Technical guidance for the IT team" vs "Talking points for the exit meeting with management" will all require different tones.
Anatomy

Anatomy of an Effective Prompt

Every strong audit prompt contains these components. Missing even one can significantly reduce output quality.

Component Purpose Example
Role/Persona Sets expertise level and perspective "Act as an experienced internal auditor..."
Context Provides background information "I am planning an audit of accounts payable at a public university..."
Task Clearly states what you need "Identify 8-10 inherent risks organized by category..."
Format Specifies desired output structure "Present as a numbered list with risk descriptions and ratings..."
Constraints Sets boundaries or requirements "Focus on risks specific to higher education. Maximum 500 words."
Examples Shows desired output style "Similar to: Risk: Duplicate payments. Likelihood: Medium. Impact: High."
Techniques

Four Prompting Techniques Every Auditor Should Know

Match your prompting technique to your task complexity for the best results.

🎯
Zero-Shot Prompting
Ask directly without providing examples. Works well for straightforward, well-defined tasks where the AI already understands what is needed.
✅
Best For
Simple, clearly defined tasks. Generating lists of risks. Creating basic interview questions. Summarizing documents you've pasted in.
💡
Example
"List five common fraud risks in accounts payable."
📝
Few-Shot Prompting
Provide 2–4 examples of the desired output format within your prompt. The AI learns the pattern and applies it to your new request.
✅
Best For
Formatting consistency — when you need outputs to match a specific style. Writing finding titles, risk ratings, or procedure steps in a consistent format.
💡
Example
"Write a finding title following these examples:
'Incomplete Vendor Due Diligence Process'
'Missing Segregation of Duties in Cash Handling'

Now write a title for: 15% of sampled purchase orders were approved after goods were received."
🔗
Chain-of-Thought
Ask the AI to work through problems step-by-step, showing its reasoning. Dramatically improves accuracy for complex analysis tasks.
✅
Best For
Complex control evaluations, root cause analysis, risk assessment frameworks, and any task requiring structured reasoning rather than just content generation.
💡
Example
"Analyze whether this control is effective. Think step by step:
1. Identify the risk this control addresses
2. Assess design adequacy
3. Consider failure points
4. Provide your conclusion"
🔄
Iterative Refinement
Start with a basic prompt, review the output, then use follow-up prompts to improve it. Often the most effective approach for complex deliverables.
✅
Best For
Complete audit findings, full reports, detailed work programs. Anything that benefits from multiple passes and refinement based on your professional judgment.
💡
Refinement Sequence
Initial: "Draft an audit finding about missing approvals."
→ Refinement 1: "Add more specific quantification of impact."
→ Refinement 2: "Strengthen the recommendation to be more actionable."
Mistakes to Avoid

5 Common Prompting Mistakes

Mistake The Problem Better Approach
Too Vague "Write something about controls" "Write a control description for the three-way match process in accounts payable"
No Context "Is this a good control?" "Given this control [description] designed to prevent [risk], evaluate its design adequacy"
Too Many Tasks "Write finding, recommendation, get management response, and rate risk" Break into separate prompts for each task
Assuming Memory "Update the finding per our discussion" AI has no memory of your discussion — provide the specific changes needed
No Format Guidance "Give me some interview questions" "Provide 10 open-ended interview questions in numbered list format"
💡

Instead of {{audit_area}} = "payroll", try "payroll processing for 2,500 employees including faculty, staff, and student workers with bi-weekly and monthly pay cycles."

Chapter Six
Quality Assurance & Professional Judgment
+75 XP
Human-AI Partnership

AI Augments. You Decide.

AI is a tool to augment, not replace, professional judgment. You remain responsible for all work product quality, regardless of AI assistance. Think of AI as a capable research assistant who can draft content quickly but needs your expert review and refinement.

✔️
Verify All Factual Claims
Especially policy citations and regulatory references. AI may confidently cite standards that do not exist. Always cross-check against authoritative sources.
🔎
Apply Professional Skepticism
Apply the same skepticism to AI suggestions that you would to management assertions. The fact that AI generated it does not make it correct.
🧠
Maintain Your Expertise
Understand the methodology behind prompts. Attempt tasks manually occasionally. Use methodology notes as learning opportunities, not shortcuts.
📁
Document AI Use
Note in workpapers when AI tools were used. Retain significant prompts and outputs if required. Document verification steps performed on AI output.

Prompt Chaining

Building a Complete Finding Step by Step

Complex audit tasks benefit from breaking work into sequential prompts where each output feeds into the next — the "prompt chain" approach consistently outperforms single comprehensive prompts.

1
Analyze
"Given these test results [data], identify the key control weakness and its root cause."
2
Quantify
"Based on the control weakness identified, help me quantify the potential impact in terms of financial exposure and operational risk."
3
Research
"What are best practices for addressing this type of control weakness in higher education?"
4
Draft
"Using the analysis above, draft a finding in five-attribute format targeting an Audit Committee audience."
Refinement Strategies

When the Output Isn't What You Needed

Use these refinement prompts to improve AI outputs that miss the mark.

Problem with Output Use This Refinement Prompt
Too generic "Make this more specific to [industry/context]. Include concrete examples."
Too long "Condense this to [X] words/paragraphs while keeping key points."
Missing elements "Add [specific element]. Also address [missing topic]."
Wrong tone "Rewrite for [audience] audience. Use [formal/conversational] tone."
Needs structure "Reorganize into [format type] with clear [sections/categories]."
Too theoretical "Make this actionable. Include specific steps and responsible parties."

QUESTION 01 OF 06
What does the "C" in the CRAFT framework stand for?
✅ Correct! Context provides the AI with relevant background information about your role, engagement type, organization, and constraints. Without context, the AI gives generic responses instead of audit-specific ones.
QUESTION 02 OF 06
An AI confidently tells you that "IIA Standard 14.7 requires quarterly fraud risk assessments." What should you do?
✅ Correct! This is hallucination — LLMs can generate confident-sounding but completely false citations. Standard 14.7 may not even exist. Always verify policy references and standard citations against authoritative sources before including them in workpapers.
QUESTION 03 OF 06
Which prompting technique is best when you need consistent output formatting, like writing 10 finding titles that all follow the same style?
✅ Correct! Few-Shot Prompting works best for formatting consistency. By providing 2–3 examples of desired output style, you teach the AI the exact pattern to follow, ensuring all 10 finding titles match your preferred format.
QUESTION 04 OF 06
You paste raw transaction data with employee names and account numbers into an AI tool to analyze for fraud. What's the key concern?
✅ Correct! Data protection is the primary concern. Before entering any audit data into AI tools, verify your organization's policies on what data is permitted. Always anonymize sensitive information and use enterprise versions with appropriate data handling agreements.
QUESTION 05 OF 06
Which prompt library tab should you use during the fieldwork phase to design tests of operating effectiveness?
✅ Correct! FIELD-002 specifically supports designing tests to verify that controls are operating effectively during the fieldwork phase (IIA Standards 14.1–14.6). FU-001 is for validating management's remediation after the audit, not during fieldwork.
QUESTION 06 OF 06
A new staff auditor relies entirely on AI-generated prompts and never attempts tasks manually. What is the main professional development concern?
✅ Correct! Over-reliance is a genuine concern. While AI accelerates work, it can hinder professional development if auditors never build foundational skills independently. The manual recommends: attempting tasks manually occasionally, understanding the methodology behind prompts, and using methodology notes as learning opportunities — not just shortcuts.

Appendix C

Glossary of Key Terms

Master the language of AI-assisted auditing.

Chain-of-Thought Prompting
A technique where the AI is asked to show its reasoning step-by-step, improving accuracy for complex analytical tasks.
Context Window
The amount of text an LLM can process at once, measured in tokens. Limits how much information can be included in a single conversation.
Few-Shot Prompting
Providing examples of desired outputs within a prompt to guide the AI's response format and quality.
Hallucination
When an AI generates confident-sounding but factually incorrect information, including fake citations or invented facts. Always verify.
Iterative Refinement
The process of improving AI outputs through sequential follow-up prompts that build on previous responses toward the desired deliverable.
Knowledge Cutoff
The date after which an LLM has no training data, limiting its awareness of recent events, regulation changes, or policy updates.
Large Language Model (LLM)
An AI system trained on vast text data to understand and generate human-like language. The technology behind today's AI tools.
Prompt Engineering
The skill of crafting effective prompts to achieve desired, audit-ready outputs from AI systems consistently and reliably.
Prompt Library
A curated collection of pre-written, tested prompts organized for specific audit use cases and aligned with IIA Standards.
Token
The basic unit of text processing for LLMs; roughly equivalent to a word or word fragment. Context windows are measured in tokens.
Variable / Placeholder
A marker in a prompt template (like {{audit_area}}) indicating where engagement-specific information should be inserted.
Zero-Shot Prompting
Asking an AI to perform a task without providing examples, relying entirely on the model's training data and the quality of your instructions.
